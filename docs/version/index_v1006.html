People and Communities in Western Sydney

In this document, we will take you through the demographic, socioeconomic, and health profiles of communities in Western Sydney from 2011 to 2021.


Data Sources

The analysis is based on Census data from 2011 and 2021, accessed through the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) TableBuilder.

Welcome to Sydney, Australia!

This is a city map.

The blue marker highlights the iconic Sydney Opera House, an enduring symbol of Sydney that attracts visitors from all over the world.


From the Sydney Opera House, about 30 kilometres to the west, lies Western Sydney, a region rising rapidly.

The red marker points to Parramatta CBD, which is the vibrant heart of Western Sydney.

Next, we will tell you a story about Western Sydney and its people.

Let’s begin with a brief introduction to Western Sydney.

This is a partial map of New South Wales.

Let’s turn our attention to the areas outlined in purple. They represent the 6 NSW Metropolitan Local Health Districts:

  • Nepean Blue Mountains
  • Northern Sydney
  • South Eastern Sydney
  • South Western Sydney
  • Sydney
  • Western Sydney

These 6 LHDs form the healthcare network of metropolitan NSW, providing health services to the people living in this region.

The orange area represents WSLHD, recognised as a leader in clinical care, research, and education across Western Sydney.

Let’s take a closer look at the population shifts in metropolitan NSW from 2011 to 2021.

According to 2011 Census, around 4.14 million people lived in the metropolitan region, accounting for about 60% of the entire NSW population.

By 2021, that metropolitan population had grown to almost 5 million, accounting for more than 60% of the entire NSW population.

Western Sydney and South Western Sydney alone each passed the milestone of 1 million residents.

Western Sydney’s population growth rate alone has reached closely 30%.

Next, let’s break down the metropolitan region in more detail.

Each Local Health District can be mapped using Statistical Area Level 2 (SA2) boundaries.


Defined by the ABS, an SA2 represents a community where people live, work, and interact socially and economically.


While SA2s do not always fit neatly within a single LHD, together they provide a useful way to examine communities at a smaller geographic scale.

We calculated population growth rates by SA2. From this, we discovered that…

We identified high-grwoth SA2s with growth rate exceeding 100%.

Also, we found that most of high-grwoth SA2s are concentrated in Western Sydney.

Next, we focus on the high-grwoth SA2s within Western Sydney.

Back in 2011, their populations were all under 10,000. Over just ten years, these communities grew at an extraordinary pace.


This rapid change signals that WSLHD is full of fascinating and valuable patterns for us to explore…

Now, let’s break down WSLHD.

WSLHD is responsible for delivering public healthcare across 4 local government areas (LGAs):


  • Blacktown
  • The Hills Shire
  • Parramatta
  • Cumberland

This is Blacktown.


Blacktown is the largest LGA in WSLHD by population, and the fastest-growing one as well.

In just a decade, its population jumped from over 300K to more than 400K, marking an impressive growth rate of over 30%.


This is The Hills Shire.


The Hills Shire is the largest LGA in WSLHD by land size, and the one that grew the fastest between 2011 and 2021, with its population soaring by 33%.


This is Parramatta.


Parramatta is the CBD of Western Sydney and the second largest LGA in WSLHD by population.

Between 2011 and 2021, it rose from the third to the second most populous LGA in WSLHD, with its population surpassing 200K.


This is Cumberland.


Cumberland was formerly known as Holroyd before being renamed in 2016. Between 2011 and 2021, its population grew steadily, surpassing the 200k mark by 2021.


let’s explore how the populations of the four LGAs have grown between 2011 and 2021.

We now integrate the population bar charts of the four LGAs for a comparative view.

But that’s not enough…

If we break the population down into four age groups:


  • Children & Teens (0–19)
  • Young Adults (20–39)
  • Middle-aged Adults (40–59)
  • Elderly People (60+)

An interesting pattern begins to emerge…

What we see is a surge of young adults flowing into Western Sydney, becoming the driving force of the region’s population.


Across all four LGAs, young adults grew by about 30%, reaching more than 350K people in total.

Alongside the growth of the young workforce, there has also been a significant increase in the number of children and teenagers, reflecting higher birth rates.


This suggests that young families represent the dominant household profile in Western Sydney.

But there’s another striking pattern…

In just ten years, the 60+ population grew by over 40% in total, the highest increase of all age groups.


In Blacktown and The Hills Shire, the growth rate even topped 50%, a clear sign of the healthcare challenges already confronting WSLHD.

we’ve seen a surge of young workers flowing into Western Sydney. We believe this is strongly connected to the arrival of new migrants.

Now, let’s dive deeper into the cultural diversity.

First, we look at the country of birth distribution by metropolitan LHDs.

In 2021, 47.0% of WSLHD’s population was born overseas, the highest proportion among all metropolitan LHDs.

However, not only the country of birth.

Also, the languages speaking at home show a similar proportion.

In 2021, non-English native speakers made up more than 50% of WSLHD.

The figures highlight Western Sydney as the most culturally diverse part of metropolitan NSW.

This pattern also makes us more interested in the migrant structure of Western Sydney, so we continue to break down WSLHD.

Across WSLHD, Cumberland and Parramatta have a majority overseas-born population (~52–53%).


Blacktown is roughly balanced (~50/50), while The Hills Shire is more Australian-born (~58%).

Home-language patterns mirror this:


Non-English is the majority in Cumberland and Parramatta (55–65%), near parity in Blacktown, and English-dominant in The Hills Shire (~59%).

But what we really want to know is: who are the main migrant communities in Western Sydney?

Here we see the top 20 overseas countries of birth for WSLHD residents in 2021.

India leads by a large margin, with China and the Philippines next.

Other countries like South Korea, Nepal, Lebanon, and Sri Lanka add to the rich mix of communities across Western Sydney.

And…

Also, we identified the SA2s where the overseas-born population exceeds WSLHD’s overall rate of 47.0%.

These hotspots are largely clustered in Cumberland and Parramatta.

Similarly, non-English languages speaking at home show a comparable pattern.

Mandarin, Arabic, Cantonese, and Hindi are leading the way.

Punjabi, Korean, Tamil, Tagalog, and Urdu are also commonly speaking at home.

In addition…

The share of residents speaking a non-English language at home (above WSLHD’s overall 50.7%) follows a similar pattern.

The SA2s with high proportions are clustered in Cumberland and Parramatta.
